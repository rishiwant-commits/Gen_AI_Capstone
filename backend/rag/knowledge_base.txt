NITROGEN DEFICIENCY
Low nitrogen levels reduce vegetative growth, cause yellowing of leaves, and significantly lower crop yield.
Nitrogen is essential for chlorophyll production and protein synthesis in plants.
Apply urea (46-0-0) at 100-150 kg/ha to correct nitrogen deficiency in wheat and rice crops.
Split nitrogen application into two doses — at sowing and at tillering stage — for better absorption.
Organic sources like farmyard manure and green manure also help restore nitrogen levels over time.

PHOSPHORUS DEFICIENCY
Low phosphorus reduces root development, delays maturity, and lowers grain quality.
Phosphorus is critical for energy transfer and root elongation in all cereal crops.
Apply DAP (Di-Ammonium Phosphate) at 100 kg/ha as a basal dose before sowing to correct phosphorus deficiency.
Single super phosphate (SSP) is a cost-effective alternative for phosphorus supplementation.
Phosphorus availability is highest at soil pH between 6.0 and 7.0.

POTASSIUM DEFICIENCY
Low potassium weakens plant cell walls, reduces disease resistance, and causes poor grain filling.
Potassium improves drought tolerance and water use efficiency in crops like maize and wheat.
Apply muriate of potash (MOP) at 60-80 kg/ha to restore potassium levels.
Potassium deficiency symptoms include scorching of leaf margins and weak stems.
Sandy soils are more prone to potassium leaching and require more frequent application.

SOIL pH — ACIDIC CONDITIONS
Soil pH below 5.5 causes aluminum and manganese toxicity, which inhibits root growth.
Acidic soils reduce availability of phosphorus, calcium, and magnesium to plants.
Apply agricultural lime (calcium carbonate) at 2-4 tonnes/ha to raise soil pH.
Dolomitic lime also supplies magnesium along with correcting acidity.
Liming should be done 4-6 weeks before sowing for best results.

SOIL pH — ALKALINE CONDITIONS
Soil pH above 7.5 causes iron, zinc, and manganese deficiency in crops.
Alkaline soils are common in arid and semi-arid regions with low rainfall.
Apply gypsum (calcium sulfate) at 2-3 tonnes/ha to reduce soil pH gradually.
Elemental sulfur at 200-500 kg/ha is effective for strongly alkaline soils.
Organic matter addition helps buffer soil pH and improve nutrient availability.

SOIL FERTILITY AND ORGANIC CARBON
Organic carbon below 0.5% indicates poor soil health and low microbial activity.
Low organic matter reduces water retention, nutrient cycling, and soil structure.
Apply farmyard manure at 10-15 tonnes/ha or compost at 5-8 tonnes/ha to improve organic carbon.
Green manuring with legumes like dhaincha or sunhemp adds nitrogen and organic matter simultaneously.
Crop residue incorporation instead of burning significantly improves organic carbon over 2-3 seasons.

RAINFALL AND IRRIGATION
Low rainfall below 500 mm per season causes water stress, wilting, and yield loss.
Water stress during flowering and grain filling stages is most damaging to yield.
Drip irrigation improves water use efficiency by 40-50% compared to flood irrigation.
Sprinkler irrigation is suitable for wheat, maize, and vegetables in water-scarce regions.
Mulching with crop residues reduces soil moisture evaporation by up to 30%.
Rainwater harvesting and farm ponds help buffer against erratic rainfall patterns.

HEAT STRESS
Temperatures above 38°C during flowering cause pollen sterility and poor grain set.
Heat stress accelerates crop maturity, reducing the grain filling period and final yield.
Use heat-tolerant varieties such as HD-3086 for wheat or DRR Dhan 42 for rice in high-temperature zones.
Irrigate crops during evening hours to reduce canopy temperature during heat waves.
Mulching and intercropping with shade-providing plants can reduce soil surface temperature.

WHEAT CROP MANAGEMENT
Wheat grows best at temperatures between 15-25°C during vegetative stage and 20-25°C during grain filling.
Optimal soil pH for wheat is 6.0-7.5 with well-drained loamy or clay-loam soils.
Wheat requires 450-650 mm of water over the growing season.
Recommended NPK for wheat: 120-60-40 kg/ha for irrigated conditions.
Sowing in Rabi season (October-November) gives best yields in North India.
Common wheat diseases include rust, powdery mildew, and loose smut — use resistant varieties.

RICE CROP MANAGEMENT
Rice thrives in warm, humid conditions with temperatures between 20-35°C.
Paddy requires 1000-2000 mm of water and grows best in clay or clay-loam soils with good water retention.
Recommended NPK for rice: 100-50-50 kg/ha under transplanted conditions.
System of Rice Intensification (SRI) can reduce water use by 25-50% while maintaining yield.
Kharif season (June-July transplanting) is optimal for rice in most Indian regions.
Blast, bacterial blight, and brown planthopper are major rice pests — monitor regularly.

MAIZE CROP MANAGEMENT
Maize requires temperatures between 18-32°C and well-drained loamy soils with pH 5.8-7.0.
Maize is sensitive to waterlogging — ensure proper field drainage.
Recommended NPK for maize: 150-75-75 kg/ha with split nitrogen application.
Maize needs 500-800 mm of water, with critical periods at tasseling and silking stages.
Kharif maize (June-July sowing) and Rabi maize (October-November) are both viable in India.
Fall armyworm is a major emerging pest in maize — use pheromone traps for early detection.

IRRIGATION TYPES
Drip irrigation delivers water directly to the root zone, minimizing evaporation and weed growth.
Sprinkler irrigation is suitable for uneven terrain and crops like wheat, groundnut, and vegetables.
Flood irrigation is the most common but least efficient method, suitable for rice and sugarcane.
Rainfed farming depends entirely on monsoon and is high-risk in low-rainfall zones.
Micro-irrigation adoption can increase water productivity by 30-50% in water-scarce regions.

SEASONAL GUIDANCE
Kharif crops (sown June-July, harvested October-November): rice, maize, cotton, soybean, groundnut.
Rabi crops (sown October-November, harvested March-April): wheat, barley, mustard, chickpea.
Zaid crops (sown March-April, harvested June): watermelon, cucumber, muskmelon, fodder crops.
Crop rotation between Kharif and Rabi seasons helps break pest cycles and improve soil health.
Intercropping legumes with cereals improves nitrogen fixation and reduces fertilizer costs.

REGIONAL GUIDANCE
North India (Punjab, Haryana, UP): wheat-rice rotation dominates; focus on water table management.
South India (AP, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu): rice, maize, and pulses; red and black soils common.
East India (Bihar, West Bengal, Odisha): high rainfall; rice dominant; flooding risk management critical.
West India (Maharashtra, Gujarat, Rajasthan): dryland farming; drought-resistant varieties essential.
Central India (MP, Chhattisgarh): soybean-wheat rotation; black cotton soils with high water retention.

SOIL TYPES
Loamy soil: ideal for most crops; good water retention and drainage; high fertility.
Clay soil: high water retention; suitable for rice and sugarcane; prone to waterlogging.
Sandy soil: low water and nutrient retention; requires frequent irrigation and fertilization.
Red soil: low nitrogen and phosphorus; suitable for groundnut, millets, and pulses with amendments.
Black soil (Vertisol): high clay content; excellent for cotton and soybean; cracks when dry.
